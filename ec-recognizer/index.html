Guide

True sky view. The observer is at the center of a hemisphere. The program uses a full-screen stereographic sky-map style projection of the visible hemisphere, so the sky now fills the screen instead of living inside a default circle.

Altitude. Smaller conductor curves are closer to the zenith. Larger conductor curves move toward the horizon.

Longitude. Longitude comes from the equalized percentile of frac(log₂ Ω), where Ω is the real period. Equalization prevents one longitude from becoming too crowded.

Prominence. The largest prime factor of the conductor is emphasized continuously. Curves whose conductor involves only very small primes appear much brighter and larger.

Shape. Torsion controls the geometric star core and ray pattern. Rank controls the main color family. CM curves keep a gold signature.

LOD. Small stars stay as luminous points. Small stars remain luminous points. Up to about 30–40 prominent stars reveal torsion geometry and short rays; the selected star always reveals full detail and remains selected while you drag the sky. Color depends only on rank and torsion; torsion controls distinct ray/shape families for cyclic [n] versus product [2,2n], while rank adds extra rings and CM adds a gold signature.

Controls. Wheel or +/- zooms without a practical upper limit. Drag pans the sky locally like a sky-map application. The visible-star control uses coarse levels for steadier movement. 0 resets. Esc closes the panel. Tiles load lazily.
